Nascent Chinese
Literary Movement

Zhou Zuoren, who was the younger brother of the renowned writer Zhou Shuren (literary name Lu Xun), received a classical education. In 1906 the two brothers went to Japan, where Zhou Zuoren studied Japanese language and literature, Classical Greek literature, and English literature.
Writing essays in vernacular Chinese for the influential magazine La Jeunesse, Zhou was a key figure in the May Fourth Movement. He was an advocate of literary reform. In a 1918 article, he called for a "humanist literature" in which "any custom or rule that goes against human instincts and nature should be rejected or rectified". Unlike his brother who write with deep emotion and sharp words, his writing style is soft and close to the everydaylife. A lot of his essay also shows his studies in easter and western culture. His short essays, with their refreshing style, won him many readers up to the present day.

Shino-Japan War

During the Shino-Japan war, while most of the professor of Peiking University left Beijing, the principle of Peiking University Jiang Mengling in his memoris wrote that ask Zhou to stay in Beijing. Because of Zhou’s unique relationship with Japanese, he entrusted Zhou to take care of the University. During the eight years of Japanese occupation, the University buildings were not damaged, and even occured more equiptment.
In the begining of Japanese occupation, Zhou published a aticle on the newpaper that criticized the invasion of Japan. After he published the article, was assassinated by a mysterious student. Both Japanese and Nationalist party refuse to acknowlege the criminal. After the assassination, Japanese army sent soldier to Zhou’s household, which increased people’s doubt about Zhou’s loyalty to country and relationship with Japan.

Translantion Works

Most of his translations are pioneering, which include a collection of Greek mimes, Sappho's lyrics, Euripides' tragedies, Kojiki, Shikitei Sanba's Ukiyoburo, Sei Shōnagon's Makura no Sōshi and a collection of Kyogen. He considered his translation of Lucian's Dialogues, which he finished late in his life, as his greatest literary achievement. He was also the first one to translate (from English) the story Ali Baba into Chinese (known as Xianü Nu 侠女奴). After the war Zhou focused on translation Ancient Greek literature and classical Japanese literature, one of his representative work was The Pillow Book(枕草子), an ancient Japanese non-fiction that depict the life of andcient Japanese inner court life. Zhou's version was praised for caputring the core of Japanese language.

Later In Life

Because he remained in Beijing during the Sino-Japanese War (1937–45) and worked for a Japanese-sponsored bureau of education, Zhou was tried as a collaborator by the National Government after the war ended and was condemned to death. He received a full pardon in 1949, which permitted him to continue his research. After the communist takeover that same year, he returned to Beijing, where he continued to write and translate. In 1966, the Cultural Revolution started and Zhou became one of the target of percecution. He died in May 1967 because of illness.
